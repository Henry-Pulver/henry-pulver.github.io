The Need for Scalable Assessment

Attitudes towards online education are shifting - more people than ever are going online for their education. Thanks to the ease of online distribution, expositional content - lectures, videos, notes, etc. - scales well, so there is no limit on the number of students. However, methods for quality scalable online assessment have not kept up with the pace.

The scalable assessment methods prevalent in massive open online courses (MOOCs) are fundamentally flawed. MOOCs most commonly use algorithmic assessment which reduces the depth of understanding that can be explored. Also, it makes cheating child’s play, which devalues the qualifications received.

Next most popular is peer-assessment. This decreases the quality of feedback and the reliability of the assessment as assessors aren’t subject experts. For these reasons, both peer and automated assessment are inadequate for reputable accreditation or grading. MOOC course directors report that this is a key obstacle for MOOCs.

In contrast, human assessment allows course directors to create challenging, thought-provoking questions. Conceptual questions that require argumentation, creativity and design usually reinforce students’ learning the most. Human assessment also enables personalised and informative feedback. However, the number of students who can access this is limited as assessors are restricted to the course director’s students and colleagues.

Our Solution

Our solution is an online gig-economy marketplace that will facilitate scalable human assessment whilst benefiting assessors. This would connect freelance, subject-specialist assessors with course directors through an online platform.

As well as earning generous rates, being an assessor provides opportunities for personal and professional growth. Assessing work and giving feedback is a powerful way of developing subject mastery [1]. It’s also a fantastic opportunity to build and demonstrate teaching experience. Our assessors do all this while working flexibly - from wherever they like and on their schedule!

Anyone with a degree, or who is currently in higher education, can register as an assessor.

To ensure assessments can be trusted, we developed a robust statistical model to calibrate the marks, flag unreliable assessors, and adjust assessor rates. By double-marking some student scripts, assessors’ relative consistency and bias can be inferred and controlled for. This also allows the most exceptional assessors to recognised and rewarded.
