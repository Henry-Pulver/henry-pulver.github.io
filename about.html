The Need for Scalable Assessment

Attitudes towards online education are shifting - more people than ever are going online for their education. Methods for scalable online assessment, however, have not kept up with the pace.

The scalable assessment methods prevalent in massive open online courses (MOOCs) are fundamentally flawed. MOOCs most commonly use algorithmic assessment which severely limits the types of questions that can be asked. Also, cheating is trivial. This brings into question the respect held for qualifications assessed algorithmically.

Next most popular is peer-assessment. Here questions can be chosen slightly more flexibly, but the quality of the assessment and feedback is limited. For these reasons, both peer and automated assessment are inadequate for reputable accreditation or grading. MOOC course directors report that this is a key obstacle for MOOCs.

In contrast, human assessment allows course directors to flexibly create challenging, thought-provoking questions. Conceptual questions that require argumentation, diagrams, design and creativity usually reinforce students’ learning the most. Human assessment also enables personalised and informative feedback.

Thanks to the ease of online distribution, expositional content - lectures, videos, notes, etc. - scales well to arbitrarily large cohorts. In contrast, human assessment and feedback from subject experts currently doesn’t as the assessor pool is typically limited to the course director’s students and colleagues.

Our Solution

We believe that an online gig-economy marketplace could facilitate scalable human assessment whilst benefitting assessors. This would connect freelance, subject-specialist assessors with course directors through an online platform.

As well as earning generous wages, being an assessor has many opportunities for personal and professional growth. Assessing work and giving feedback is a powerful way of developing subject mastery [1]. It’s also a fantastic opportunity to build and demonstrate teaching experience. Our assessors do all this while working flexibly - from wherever they like and on their schedule!

Anyone with a degree, or who is currently in higher education, can register as an assessor.

To ensure assessments can be trusted, we developed a robust statistical model to calibrate the marks, flag unreliable assessors, and adjust assessor rates. This will require double-marking some student scripts so assessors’ relative consistency and bias can be inferred and controlled for. This also allows us to recognise and reward the most exceptional assessors.
